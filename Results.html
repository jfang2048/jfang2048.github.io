Results

Pollutant change, land-cover transitions, and population exposure

All input layers were reprojected to WGS84 (EPSG:4326) and clipped to Germany. The processing follows the eight-step GIS pipeline defined in Labs 2–4: monthly and annual pollutant maps (Steps 1–2), EU threshold concentration classification (Step 3), Annual Mean Aggregation Change 2021–2023 (Step 4), Land Cover Change detection with binary change masks (Step 5), zonal statistics correlating pollutant change with land-cover transitions (Step 6), pollutant-population exposure mapping (Step 7), and dissolved chart layers for exposure summaries (Step 8).

Steps 1–2: Pollutant Annual Averages, 2023

Monthly CAMS reanalysis rasters were aggregated to annual means for NO₂, PM₂.₅, and PM₁₀ using r.series. The table below summarises the valid-pixel statistics across Germany.

Step 3: EU Concentration Classification

Annual average rasters were reclassified into five concentration levels according to the EU lab thresholds (Processing Toolbox → Reclassify by table). The thresholds differ by pollutant because each has distinct health guideline values: NO₂ 40 µg/m³, PM₂.₅ 25 µg/m³, PM₁₀ 40 µg/m³ annual limit.

Germany falls within the lower concentration classes: NO₂ and PM₁₀ contain classes 1–2; PM₂.₅ contains classes 2–3 (class 3 appears in only 2 valid raster pixels).

Step 4: Annual Mean Aggregation Change (AMAC), 2021–2023

The bi-temporal difference AMAC = A2023 − A2021 was computed per pollutant using the Raster Calculator. This step quantifies the independent spatial evolution of air pollutants before integrating land-cover variables. Negative values (blue in the QML style) indicate improving air quality; positive values (red) indicate deteriorating conditions. This atmospheric change layer is cross-referenced with land-cover dynamics in Step 6.

Step 5: Land Cover Change Detection, 2021–2023

The ESRI 10 m Annual Land Cover rasters for 2021 and 2023 were combined using LCC = LC2021 × 100 + LC2023. Each student was assigned a land-cover class linked to their pollutant: NO₂ → Built Area (code 7), PM₂.₅ → Crops (code 5), PM₁₀ → Trees (code 2). Binary change masks classify each pixel as Stable (unchanged), Gain (newly added), or Loss (removed), excluding cloud-related transitions (code 10). Statistics were computed from the EPSG:3035 equal-area raster using the QGIS Raster layer unique values report. Pixel percent and area percent are computed within the Stable + Gain + Loss denominator for each assigned class.

Step 6: Zonal Statistics — Pollutant Change by Land-Cover Transition

To correlate atmospheric change with land-surface dynamics, the AMAC raster was resampled to match the ESRI 10 m resolution and zonal statistics (Mean, Minimum, Maximum; prefix Zone_) were computed within each transition zone polygon. The binary change mask was polygonised, dissolved, and used as the input vector layer. The zonal statistics layers and bar charts below quantify how pollutant concentrations changed inside Stable, Gain, and Loss areas for each assigned land-cover class.

All transition-zone means are negative, confirming that pollutant concentrations declined regardless of land-cover dynamics. Steps 7–8: Population Exposure

Step 7 combined the concentration class raster (Step 3) with WorldPop 2023 population quintile classes to create pollutant-population exposure layers (code = pol_class_max × 10 + pop_class_median). Each of the GAUL Level 2 districts received an exposure code. Step 8 dissolved these by pol_class_max, summing population counts (pop_sum) per concentration class. The formula check returned zero mismatches for all three pollutants. The charts below show the population share exposed to each concentration level.
